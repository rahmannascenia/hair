   102→Executive Summary
   103→Barendra International is a Dhaka-headquartered human hair processing and wig manufacturing enterprise that
   104→has emerged as one of Bangladesh's first formal exporters of finished human-hair products. Operating from a
   105→head office in Uttara, Sector 6, Dhaka, and a primary processing factory in Dinajpur (North Bengal), the
   106→company orchestrates a multi-stage production model that combines centralised industrial processing with a
   107→vast network of approximately 4,000-5,000 home-based female workers. The business imports raw human hair
   108→from India, Pakistan, Uzbekistan, Tajikistan, and Myanmar, supplements supply through local district-level
   109→vendors, and exports finished wigs and hair products to buyers in the United States, Europe, China, and Africa.
   110→The company's operations currently run on a combination of Microsoft Excel workbooks, hard-copy vouchers,
   111→and a small HR-tracking module, with files centralised on Google Drive. As the workforce has scaled beyond
   112→5,000 people and lot-level traceability has become essential for buyer compliance and inventory valuation, the
   113→owner has initiated a programme to implement a comprehensive Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) system.
   114→This document is the foundational business process reference that will guide that ERP implementation across
   115→five sequenced phases - beginning with accounting and stock management and culminating in export-import
   116→and letter-of-credit (LC) automation.
   117→The business follows a distinctive two-phase production model. Phase 1 distributes washed, lot-coded raw
   118→material through a five-tier hierarchy (Production Manager -> Head Leader -> Line Leader -> Home-based
   119→Supervisor -> Worker) where female workers perform hand-tool sorting and classification in their homes. Phase
   120→2 receives the half-processed material back at the Dhaka factory, where 100 in-house workers perform
   121→machine-assisted final production - sizing by length, re-washing, colour treatment, assembly, and packing -
   122→against size-wise pricing that ranges from approximately Tk 500/kg for 5-inch hair to Tk 90,000/kg for 30-inch
   123→hair.
   124→Three structural characteristics define the company's risk profile and automation priorities. First, the same lot
   125→number travels with the goods from raw material receipt to finished-goods storage, making lot traceability the
   126→backbone of both quality control and inventory valuation. Second, worker compensation is a hybrid of
   127→grade-based daily rates (A = Tk 90, B = Tk 75, C = Tk 50), attendance bonuses, and performance bonuses, all
   128→of which depend on daily quality grading by four Quality Controllers. Third, the home-based manufacturing
   129→model creates inherent tracking, theft, and skimming risks that the current manual system cannot fully mitigate -
   130→a single Line Controller may handle bulk payouts of approximately Tk 1.5 lakh for 100 workers, creating
   131→skimming exposure.
   132→This document is organised into eighteen substantive sections covering company background and industry
   133→context, organisational structure, the nine core business processes (procurement, sorting and lot creation,
   134→washing, Phase 1 distribution, quality control, payroll, half-finish return, Phase 2 final production, and
   135→sales/export), inventory and lot traceability framework, consolidated business rules, pain points and operational
   136→risks, the ERP implementation roadmap, key performance indicators, and strategic implications. Each section
   137→provides process narratives, role definitions, data flows, and the specific rules that must be encoded in the future
   138→ERP system.

   142→1. Company Background and Industry Context
   143→1.1 Company Profile
   144→Barendra International is a privately held human-hair processing and manufacturing enterprise headquartered in
   145→Uttara, Sector 6, Dhaka, with its primary industrial facility located in Dinajpur district in North Bengal. The
   146→company's founder and managing director, who hails from Naogaon district and holds a Sociology degree and
   147→an MBA from the University of Dhaka, entered the trade after four years of intensive market research across the
   148→United States, India, China, Nepal, Vietnam, and Malaysia, and received early trade mentoring from a Chinese
   149→business associate. The Dinajpur location was selected strategically, as the founder's wife originates from the
   150→region and operates a related hair-trade business there, providing established local supplier relationships and a
   151→recruitment base for the predominantly female home-based workforce.
   152→The company has been in operation for approximately five to six years and positions itself as Bangladesh's first
   153→formal exporter of finished human-hair products. It maintains a representative office in India to manage
   154→raw-material sourcing relationships and coordinates with Chinese trade counterparts for finished-goods
   155→distribution. The total workforce is estimated at 4,500-5,500 people across all sites, comprising approximately
   156→4,000-5,000 home-based female workers, 200 workers at the Dinajpur second-step factory, 100 in-house
   157→workers at the Dhaka final-production floor, and approximately 50-70 supervisory, administrative, and support
   158→staff.
   159→1.2 Product and Material Characteristics
   160→The company's sole raw material is real human hair - explicitly not synthetic fibre - which is processed into
   161→wigs, hair pieces, extensions, and gentlemen's caps for men. The distinction between real human hair and
   162→synthetic substitutes is commercially critical: real hair commands premium pricing (an identical product may
   163→sell to an African buyer for USD 5-50 but to an American buyer for USD 105, driven by quality perception and
   164→end-use), while synthetic substitutes can cause allergic skin reactions and are excluded from the company's
   165→product range. The company also imports specialised adhesive glue from the United States at USD 4,000-5,000
   166→per 100 grams, a material that cannot be substituted with counterfeits without triggering customer allergies.
   167→Two industry-specific Bengali terms describe the material states that flow through the production pipeline. Buti
   168→refers to a tightly bound, fully processed bundle that is ready for final assembly at Dhaka. Kanchi refers to a
   169→looser, longer, half-processed bundle that requires further reworking (called remik, or remake) at the Dinajpur
   170→facility before it can be promoted to the Buti state. The final wig-assembly stage is termed blended (Bengali:
   171→blended). These states - along with raw, washed, distributed, in-factory, half-finish, and finished - constitute the
   172→eight inventory buckets that the ERP system must track with real-time valuation.
   173→1.3 Industry Context and Market Position
   174→The global wigs and extensions market was valued at approximately USD 15.2 billion in 2025 and is projected
   175→to reach USD 31.13 billion by 2033, representing a compound annual growth rate of 9.6 percent (Grand View
   176→Research, retrieved 21 June 2026). North America accounts for approximately 45 percent of global demand,
   177→with the United States market alone growing from USD 2.79 billion in 2023 to a projected USD 6.34 billion by
   178→2029 at 14.69 percent CAGR (Arizton). India dominates raw-hair supply, accounting for approximately 93
   179→percent of global raw-hair exports in 2023, while China dominates finished-wig manufacturing, with the
   180→Xuchang region alone producing six of every ten wigs worldwide and employing over 300,000 workers.

   185→Plexconcil (Ministry of Commerce) explicitly acknowledges that Myanmar and Bangladesh benefit from lower
   186→labour costs and government support, allowing them to process Indian raw hair and sell finished products
   187→competitively. Bangladesh's wig and human-hair exports reached USD 19.16 million in a single fiscal quarter

   192→Barendra International's competitive position rests on three foundations: access to multi-country raw-material
   193→sourcing (reducing dependence on any single supplier), Bangladesh's low labour cost advantage (the national
   194→garment-sector minimum wage of BDT 12,500 per month, or approximately USD 113, is among the lowest in

   196→expectations around lot traceability and ethical sourcing substantiation - particularly from US and European
   197→buyers - which the current manual system cannot satisfy at scale. This pressure is a primary driver of the ERP
   198→initiative.
   199→1.4 Current Systems and Automation Gap
   200→Barendra International currently operates on Microsoft Excel workbooks and hard-copy vouchers, with files
   201→centralised on Google Drive and accessed from five to six entry-level laptops at the factory level. Two full-time
   202→data-entry operators are dedicated to transcribing the daily output of approximately 5,000 home-based workers.
   203→A small HR tracking module is used in limited fashion to monitor field supervisors' movements, but no
   204→integrated accounting, inventory, production, or payroll system exists. Internet connectivity is available at the
   205→Dhaka head office and the Dinajpur factory, with mobile hotspots used in remote field locations.
   206→The manual system creates four categories of operational pain. First, real-time stock valuation is impossible -
   207→the owner cannot instantaneously answer basic questions such as 'how much stock do I have right now, and
   208→where is it?' Second, a single transaction currently requires multiple manual entries across cash, party, and bank
   209→ledgers, creating data-reconciliation overhead and error exposure. Third, the Asset and Liability report - a basic
   210→management instrument - takes significant time to compile manually. Fourth, lot-level traceability across the
   211→40-plus-day production pipeline is extremely difficult to maintain in Excel, undermining both quality
   212→investigations and buyer compliance. These gaps collectively motivate the phased ERP implementation
   213→described in Section 16.

   272→2.2 Span-of-Control and Scaling Mathematics
   273→The Phase 1 distribution hierarchy follows a deliberate 1:5:10:10:10 multiplier. One Production Manager
   274→oversees five Head Leaders, each Head Leader oversees ten Line Leaders, each Line Leader oversees
   275→approximately ten home-based factories, and each factory hosts approximately ten workers (with documented
   276→flexibility of 9, 10, or 11 workers per factory). This mathematical structure means that a single Production
   277→Manager can theoretically coordinate 5 x 10 x 10 x 10 = 5,000 workers, which closely matches the company's
   278→actual workforce. Adding capacity therefore requires adding Head Leaders (each unlocking approximately
   279→1,000 additional workers) rather than restructuring the hierarchy.
   280→Material allocation scales inversely with hierarchy depth. A 1,200 kg washed batch received by the Production
   281→Manager is divided into four to five Head Leader allocations of 200-300 kg each. Each Head Leader divides the
   282→allocation among ten Line Leaders at approximately 30 kg each. Each Line Leader divides the 30 kg across ten
   283→factories at approximately 3 kg per factory. Each factory supervisor divides the 3 kg among ten workers at
   284→100-300 grams per worker per day. This granular allocation is the basis on which shortage and reject tracking is
   285→performed at every return step, and the ERP system must capture each transfer as a discrete inventory
   286→transaction with lot number, quantity, recipient, and timestamp.
   287→2.3 Governance and Approval Authority
   288→Approval authority is concentrated at three levels. The Production Manager approves all material distributions
   289→from the washing plant to Head Leaders and from half-finish returns into the Dhaka final production queue.
   290→Head Leaders approve daily distribution to Line Leaders and authorise worker grade entries in consultation with
   291→the Quality Controller. The Owner / Managing Director retains personal approval authority over certain stages -
   292→including supplier onboarding, large purchase orders, salary sheet final sign-off, and any out-of-tolerance
   293→shortage or reject events. This concentration of authority at the owner level is a recognised operational risk (the
   294→'owner bottleneck') that the ERP dashboard is intended to mitigate by enabling delegated decision-making with
   295→real-time visibility.

   334→3.2 Two-Phase Production Model
   335→The defining architectural feature of the business is the separation of production into two distinct phases. Phase
   336→1 encompasses all operations performed on raw or washed material through the home-based workforce -
   337→hand-tool sorting, opening, classification, and initial bundling. Phase 2 encompasses all machine-assisted and
   338→chemically intensive operations performed at the Dhaka factory - length sizing, re-washing, colour treatment,
   339→assembly, and final packing. This separation exists because the home-based environment cannot accommodate
   340→machines, chemicals, or the specialised training required for final production, while the factory environment
   341→cannot cost-effectively absorb the labour-intensive hand-sorting volume that the home-based model handles at
   342→scale.
   343→The two-phase model creates a critical intermediate inventory state - half-finished goods - that must be tracked
   344→with the same lot identity as the original raw material. Material can spend one to two weeks in Phase 1
   345→(distributed across dozens of home-based factories) before being returned to Dhaka for Phase 2. During this
   346→period, the ERP system must maintain visibility of every kilogram by lot, by Line Leader, by factory, and by
   347→individual worker, and must reconcile issued quantities against returned quantities with shortage and reject
   348→accounting at each level of the hierarchy.

   352→4. Process 1 - Raw Material Procurement
   353→4.1 Sourcing Strategy and Supplier Network
   354→Barendra International sources raw human hair from a diversified multi-country supplier network designed to
   355→reduce dependence on any single geography and to access different hair characteristics (colour, texture, length)
   356→native to each region. International sources include India (three suppliers covering the entire country), Pakistan
   357→(one supplier), Uzbekistan, Tajikistan, and Myanmar. The company also procures locally within Bangladesh
   358→through district-level vendors - deliberately not engaging at the root collection level, which would require
   359→building an extensive grassroots collection network. A representative office in India supports direct supplier
   360→relationship management and quality inspection before shipment.
   361→Raw-material pricing varies dramatically by quality tier. Standard import-grade hair is purchased at 1,000-2,000
   362→taka per kilogram, while premium grades reach 12,000-14,000 taka per kilogram. Local hair purchased from
   363→Narayanganj may be approximately 2,000 taka per kilogram cheaper than equivalent Dhaka city material. The
   364→final sized-and-processed product commands radically different prices by length: 8-inch hair sells at
   365→approximately 5,000 taka per kilogram, while 30-inch hair sells at approximately 90,000 taka per kilogram - an
   366→18-times differential that makes length retention during processing the single most important value driver in the
   367→entire pipeline.
   368→4.2 Import Process and Documentation
   369→International procurement is executed through Letters of Credit (LC) issued via the company's bank. The
   370→documentation workflow involves several bank-managed artefacts: SIT numbers (used on the export side and
   371→for dollar tracking), BTC numbers (bank tracking numbers), and LC documents prepared by the bank manager
   372→for the owner's signature. Multiple SIT numbers may be in flight simultaneously, each tracking pending dollar
   373→commitments, encashment status, and applicable export incentives. The bank can prepare invoices covering two
   374→to three parties at a time under a single SIT number, creating a many-to-many relationship between SIT
   375→numbers, suppliers, and LC instruments that the ERP system must model explicitly.
   376→Foreign-currency gain and loss must be recognised on every transaction because purchases are denominated in
   377→foreign currency (primarily US dollars) while functional currency is Bangladeshi taka. The applicable bank rate
   378→on the transaction date determines the initial recorded cost, and any subsequent exchange-rate movement
   379→between commitment and encashment creates a gain or loss that must be captured automatically. The current
   380→Excel-based system handles this manually, which is time-consuming and error-prone; the ERP accounting
   381→module (Phase 1) is specifically designed to automate this recognition.
   382→4.3 Receipt and Initial Storage
   383→Upon arrival, raw material is received at the Dhaka head office warehouse by the Store Keeper, who records the
   384→consignment against the originating LC and SIT documentation. The material is immediately entered into the
   385→raw-material stock bucket with a valuation that includes the LC cost, landing charges, customs duty, and
   386→applicable bank charges. This initial valuation is the foundation for all subsequent cost roll-ups through the
   387→production pipeline - any error or omission at this stage propagates through to finished-goods cost and margin
   388→calculations. The ERP system must therefore enforce a complete cost-capture checklist at the goods-receipt
   389→step.

   399→5. Process 2 - Initial Sorting and Lot Creation
   400→5.1 Colour Separation
   401→Upon receipt and initial storage at the Dhaka warehouse, raw material enters the colour separation process. This
   402→is a manual, visually judgement-based operation in which workers physically separate hair by natural colour
   403→into distinct groupings. The industry uses a universal 1-to-10 level system for hair colour (where 1 is black and
   404→10 is lightest blonde), but Barendra International's internal colour codes include specific identifiers such as 600
   405→and 627, which may correspond to either colour codes or supplier lot references. Colour separation is the first
   406→quality-determining step because subsequent processing (washing, chemical treatment) is calibrated to colour
   407→characteristics, and mixing colours at this stage creates defects that cannot be economically recovered later.
   408→5.2 Lot and Batch Number Assignment
   409→After colour separation, each colour-separated batch is assigned a unique lot (or batch) number. Critically, lots
   410→are created based on the goods themselves - the lot is defined by the material it contains, not by an arbitrary
   411→numbering scheme. A lot and the goods it contains can be the same thing. This goods-based lot creation
   412→principle is fundamental to the entire traceability framework because it ensures that every subsequent
   413→transaction, transformation, and movement can be traced back to a specific physical batch of raw material with
   414→known colour, supplier origin, and cost characteristics.
   415→The lot numbering system supports sub-lots: a single main lot (for example, a 120-kilogram batch) can contain
   416→multiple colour-wise sub-lots (for example, colour 600 plus colour 627) under the same main lot number. Lots
   417→can also be merged - two existing lots can be combined into a new lot - or material can be transferred from one
   418→lot to another when physical consolidation makes operational sense. These lot operations must be explicitly
   419→modelled in the ERP system as discrete transactions with audit trails, because the same physical hair may
   420→change lot identity during its lifecycle while remaining fully traceable to its original raw-material receipt.
   421→5.3 Voucher-to-Lot Relationship
   422→Material movement is documented through delivery vouchers, and the relationship between vouchers and lots is
   423→many-to-many. A single delivery voucher can contain multiple lots (for example, voucher 946 may contain
   424→several lots), and a single lot can span multiple vouchers as it is distributed in tranches. This many-to-many
   425→relationship is a critical data-modelling requirement for the ERP system: both the voucher entity and the lot
   426→entity must exist as first-class objects, with a junction table linking them and capturing the quantity of each lot
   427→represented in each voucher. The current Excel system cannot adequately represent this relationship, which is a
   428→primary source of traceability gaps.

   441→6. Process 3 - Washing and Preparation
   442→After lot creation in Dhaka, material is transported to the Dinajpur factory - the company's primary industrial
   443→processing facility - for washing and preparation. The Dinajpur site houses the washing plant (staffed by 5-7
   444→people including a floor manager and washing-plant lead), accounts staff (5 people), and 200 second-step
   445→production workers. The washing process is the first industrial transformation applied to the raw material and
   446→serves multiple purposes: it removes impurities accumulated during collection and transit, prepares the hair for
   447→colour separation consistency, and establishes the baseline weight against which all subsequent shortage and
   448→reject calculations are measured.
   449→6.1 Wash, Dry, and Restock Cycle
   450→The washing cycle comprises four sequential steps. First, the hair is washed using shampoo and chemical
   451→treatments calibrated to the colour and quality characteristics recorded at lot creation. Second, the washed hair
   452→is dried using controlled methods to prevent damage and to standardise moisture content. Third, the dried hair is
   453→reweighed and restocked with a new recorded quantity. Fourth, the restocked material is forwarded to the
   454→Production Manager for distribution into Phase 1 field manufacturing.
   455→Weight loss during washing is a normal and expected outcome - typically 2-3 kilograms per batch - driven by
   456→the removal of impurities, moisture loss, and minor processing waste. This weight loss must be recorded as a
   457→formal inventory adjustment against the lot, with the new restocked quantity becoming the baseline for all
   458→subsequent distribution and reconciliation. Failure to capture this adjustment accurately creates phantom
   459→shortages downstream that cannot be explained or resolved. The ERP system must therefore enforce a
   460→weight-capture step at the end of washing, with the difference between pre-wash and post-wash quantities
   461→automatically calculated and posted to a washing-loss account.
   462→6.2 Handoff to Production Manager
   463→Once restocked, washed material is handed off to the Production Manager, who becomes the single point of
   464→accountability for Phase 1 distribution. A typical handoff batch is approximately 1,200 kilograms, which the
   465→Production Manager then divides among the five Head Leaders at 200-300 kilograms each. This handoff is a
   466→formal inventory transfer transaction in the ERP system - the material moves from the 'washed stock' bucket
   467→(held by the washing plant) to the 'distributed' bucket (held by the Production Manager), with the lot number
   468→preserved and the quantity, timestamp, and responsible party recorded. The Production Manager's stock
   469→position is then the basis for the next-level distribution to Head Leaders.

   536→7.4 Shortage, Reject, and Wastage Accounting
   537→At every return step, issued quantity is reconciled against returned quantity, and any variance is classified as
   538→either reject (quality failure - hair that cannot be used) or shortage (unaccounted loss). Industry-standard
   539→wastage is acknowledged as unavoidable - the owner notes that '100 percent of anything cannot be achieved' -
   540→but major losses are flagged for investigation. Typical Dinajpur second-step wastage runs 100-150 grams per
   541→kilogram, and final Dhaka production wastage runs 50-100 grams per kilogram. Reject material must still be
   542→returned with weight recorded, and a recovery rate (for example, 900 grams 'tikal' or recovered per 1,000 grams
   543→input) is calculated for each lot and each factory.
   544→8. Process 5 - Quality Control and Worker Grading
   545→Quality control at Barendra International operates as a continuous, daily activity rather than a discrete
   546→end-of-line inspection. Four Quality Controllers based in Dhaka are responsible for grading the output of all
   547→approximately 5,000 workers every working day, producing the A/B/C grades that directly determine each
   548→worker's daily wage. This daily grading cadence is essential because worker compensation is grade-driven - an
   549→A-grade worker earns 90 taka per day while a C-grade worker earns only 50 taka - and because the home-based
   550→manufacturing model means quality cannot be assured through process controls alone. The QC function
   551→therefore serves as both a quality gate and a payroll input.
   552→8.1 Quality Parameters and Grading Criteria
   553→Worker output is evaluated against four primary quality parameters. Length retention is the most important:
   554→after a standardised pulling test, hair that retains greater length (indicating less breakage) receives a higher
   555→grade. Breakage is directly penalised - workers whose output shows more broken hair receive lower grades
   556→because broken hair has lower commercial value. Colour correctness is visually judged against the lot's colour
   557→specification. Texture and feel are judged by hand, an experiential assessment that the QC cannot reduce to a
   558→fully objective standard. The owner acknowledges that QC judgement is approximately - but not exactly - 70
   559→percent accurate, which is an inherent limitation of the manual grading model.
   560→A critical structural feature of the grading system is lot-quality inheritance: if the raw-material lot itself is of
   561→poor quality, all workers processing that lot may receive C grades regardless of their individual effort or skill.
   562→This creates a fairness tension - workers may be penalised for lot-quality issues outside their control - and the
   563→ERP system should flag lots where the grade distribution is unusually skewed toward C so that management can
   564→distinguish lot-quality effects from worker-performance effects. Without this analytical capability, worker
   565→dissatisfaction and turnover risk increase.

   630→A defining design principle of the compensation system is that quality is rewarded over quantity. The owner

   645→Salary disbursement currently flows through bKash mobile payment, but the disbursement model introduces a

   647→workers) can total approximately 1.5 lakh taka (150,000 taka). This bulk amount is transferred to the Line
   648→Leader's bKash account, who is then responsible for distributing the correct individual amounts to each worker.
   649→The risk - explicitly acknowledged by the owner - is that the Line Leader may take more for himself and less for
   650→poorer workers, or may delay disbursement. The ERP system must address this by enabling direct bKash
   651→disbursement to individual worker accounts from the approved payroll, eliminating the Line Leader as an
   652→intermediary in the payment flow while preserving his role in distribution and supervision.

   665→10. Process 7 - Half-Finish Return and Requisition
   666→When Phase 1 home-based processing is complete for a given lot, the half-processed material must be returned
   667→from the Dinajpur field operations to the Dhaka main factory for Phase 2 final production. This return is
   668→managed through a formal requisition and booking process that preserves the original lot number end-to-end
   669→and transfers inventory custody from the field hierarchy back to the Dhaka store keeper. The return flow is a
   670→critical inventory transaction because it converts material from the 'in-factory' bucket (held by home-based
   671→factories) to the 'half-finish return' bucket (held by Dhaka), and any discrepancy between issued and returned
   672→quantities must be fully reconciled and explained.
   673→10.1 Buti versus Kanchi Material States
   674→At the half-finish stage, material is classified into one of two states that determine its next processing
   675→destination. Buti refers to tightly bound, fully processed bundles that are ready for Phase 2 final production at
   676→Dhaka - these are shipped directly to the Dhaka factory. Kanchi refers to looser, longer, half-processed bundles
   677→that require further reworking (remik) at the Dinajpur facility before they can be promoted to the Buti state and
   678→shipped to Dhaka. This state-based routing means that not all material from a given lot necessarily travels to
   679→Dhaka simultaneously - some may cycle through the Dinajpur remik process first, and the ERP system must
   680→track Buti and Kanchi as distinct sub-states within the half-finish inventory bucket.
   681→10.2 Requisition and Booking Workflow
   682→The return workflow is initiated by the Group Head (Head Leader), who raises a requisition to transfer
   683→completed half-finish goods from the field to Dhaka. The requisition references the original lot number and
   684→specifies the quantity, material state (Buti or Kanchi), and destination. The material is then 'booked' - removed
   685→from the Dinajpur field stock position and assigned a booking reference that travels with the physical
   686→consignment. Transport is handled by the company's own vehicles rather than courier services, because the high
   687→value-to-weight ratio of processed hair (a single lot can be worth approximately 1 lakh taka for 4 kilograms)
   688→makes third-party courier risk unacceptable.
   689→Upon arrival at Dhaka, the store keeper receives the consignment against the original lot number, performs a
   690→quantity verification, and posts the receipt to the 'half-finish return' inventory bucket. The lot's status is updated
   691→to reflect that it has completed Phase 1 and is now available for Phase 2 final production. The ERP system must
   692→support a 'lot status' state machine that tracks each lot through the stages of: created, washing, distributed,
   693→in-factory, half-finish-returned, in-final-production, and finished - with timestamps and responsible-party
   694→capture at each transition.

   706→11.1 Sizing - The Critical Value Driver
   707→The first and most consequential operation in Phase 2 is length sizing. Half-finish material is sorted into discrete
   708→length categories - 5-inch, 6-inch, 8-inch, 10-inch, 12-inch, 14-inch, 16-inch, 18-inch, 20-inch, and up to

   710→dramatic: 5-inch hair sells at approximately 500 taka per kilogram, 8-inch hair at approximately 5,000 taka per
   711→kilogram, and 30-inch hair at approximately 90,000 taka per kilogram. This pricing structure means that length
   712→retention throughout Phase 1 (where breakage reduces average length) is directly correlated with revenue, and
   713→that accurate sizing in Phase 2 is essential for correct invoicing and margin calculation.

   717→The sizing operation is performed in full - the company does not selectively process only small or only large
   718→sizes from a given lot. A single lot's output is distributed across the full range of size categories, with each size

   722→lot's total realisable value as the sum of (quantity in size category x rate for that category) across all size
   723→categories produced.
   724→11.2 Re-Wash, Colour Treatment, and Assembly
   725→After sizing, material may be re-washed if quality inspection indicates residual impurities from Phase 1. The
   726→re-wash uses the same shampoo and chemical treatment calibrated to the sized material's characteristics.
   727→Following re-wash, the chemist applies final colour treatment - dyes and chemical processes that produce the
   728→finished colour specification required by the buyer. This is a precision operation because colour mismatch is a
   729→primary cause of buyer rejection. After colour treatment, material proceeds to final sizing (a second size-by-size
   730→sort to verify post-colour dimensions), then to assembly (the 'blended' stage where the wig or hair piece is
   731→actually constructed using machines and hand tools), and finally to QC inspection.
   732→11.3 Final QC, Packing, and Storage
   733→The Final Quality Controller inspects each finished item against the buyer's specification (or, for stock
   734→production, against the company's internal quality standard). Approved items proceed to packing, where they
   735→are packaged size-wise with appropriate labelling and prepared for either export shipment or finished-goods
   736→storage. Packed goods are stored in the Dhaka finished-goods warehouse with the original lot number preserved
   737→as the traceability key - even at this final stage, a finished item can be traced back through Phase 2, Phase 1,
   738→washing, lot creation, and raw-material receipt to identify the original supplier, country of origin, and purchase
   739→cost. This end-to-end traceability is increasingly demanded by US and European buyers for ethical-sourcing
   740→substantiation.
   741→11.4 Specialised Material: Adhesive Glue
   742→The US-imported adhesive glue (USD 4,000-5,000 per 100 grams) is consumed during the assembly stage and
   743→warrants special inventory control. The glue cannot be substituted with counterfeits because fake adhesive
   744→causes allergic reactions in end users, which would destroy the company's reputation and expose it to buyer
   745→claims. The ERP system should track adhesive glue as a separately controlled inventory item with batch-level
   746→traceability, consumption recording against specific finished-goods lots, and automatic reorder triggers when
   747→stock falls below a defined threshold. The current Excel system treats glue as a generic consumable, which is
   748→inadequate given its unit cost and quality criticality.

   752→12. Process 9 - Sales, Export and Order Management
   753→12.1 Market and Buyer Segments
   754→Barendra International sells finished human-hair products to a diversified international buyer base spanning
   755→four major market segments. The United States is the highest-value market, with American buyers paying up to
   756→USD 105 for products that sell to African buyers at USD 5-50 - the differential reflecting quality tier, end-use
   757→application, and buyer negotiation power. Europe represents a second premium market with quality and
   758→ethical-sourcing expectations similar to the United States. China is both a competitive manufacturing centre and
   759→a market - the company coordinates with Chinese trade counterparts who distribute Bangladeshi-processed
   760→product, and the owner notes that 'almost all our work goes through them' in certain product lines. Africa
   761→represents a high-volume, lower-price segment, while local Bangladeshi sales and distribution across North
   762→Bengal districts supplement export revenue.
   763→12.2 Sales Contract and Export Process
   764→Export sales begin with a sales contract received from the buyer specifying product specifications, quantities,
   765→sizes, colours, prices, and delivery terms. The company then produces against the contract, allocating
   766→appropriate finished-goods lots to fulfil the order. Export execution mirrors the import process: LC
   767→management, SIT numbers on the export side, bank-prepared documentation, and dollar tracking of pending
   768→export receivables. Export incentives available from the bank (and from the Bangladesh Export Promotion
   769→Bureau) must be tracked and claimed. The ERP Phase 5 module - Export-Import and LC Management - is
   770→designed to automate this workflow, including document upload, SIT/BTC tracking, dollar encashment, and
   771→incentive calculation.
   772→12.3 Pricing Strategy
   773→Pricing is determined primarily by length (size-wise rate master) and quality tier, with buyer-specific
   774→negotiation applied on top. The same physical product may be priced very differently to different buyers based
   775→on their market position and willingness to pay. The owner cites the example of an African buyer paying USD
   776→5-50 for a product that commands USD 105 from an American buyer - both transactions can be profitable
   777→because the underlying cost structure (raw material plus processing) is low enough to support the lower price
   778→point, while the premium buyer's higher payment reflects brand, quality assurance, and channel value. The ERP
   779→system must support buyer-specific price lists layered on top of the size-wise rate master, with margin analysis
   780→at the buyer-and-product level.
