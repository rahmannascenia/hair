   504→   504→Barendra International is a Dhaka-headquartered human hair processing and wig manufacturing enterprise that
   505→   505→has emerged as one of Bangladesh's first formal exporters of finished human-hair products. Operating from a
   506→   506→head office in Uttara, Sector 6, Dhaka, and a primary processing factory in Dinajpur (North Bengal), the
   507→   507→company orchestrates a multi-stage production model that combines centralised industrial processing with a
   508→   508→vast network of approximately 4,000-5,000 home-based female workers. The business imports raw human hair
   509→   509→from India, Pakistan, Uzbekistan, Tajikistan, and Myanmar, supplements supply through local district-level
   510→   510→vendors, and exports finished wigs and hair products to buyers in the United States, Europe, China, and Africa.
   511→   511→The company's operations currently run on a combination of Microsoft Excel workbooks, hard-copy vouchers,
   512→   512→and a small HR-tracking module, with files centralised on Google Drive. As the workforce has scaled beyond
   513→   513→5,000 people and lot-level traceability has become essential for buyer compliance and inventory valuation, the
   514→   514→owner has initiated a programme to implement a comprehensive Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) system.
   515→   515→This document is the foundational business process reference that will guide that ERP implementation across
   516→   516→five sequenced phases - beginning with accounting and stock management and culminating in export-import
   517→   517→and letter-of-credit (LC) automation.
   518→   518→The business follows a distinctive two-phase production model. Phase 1 distributes washed, lot-coded raw
   519→   519→material through a five-tier hierarchy (Production Manager -> Head Leader -> Line Leader -> Home-based
   520→   520→Supervisor -> Worker) where female workers perform hand-tool sorting and classification in their homes. Phase
   521→   521→2 receives the half-processed material back at the Dhaka factory, where 100 in-house workers perform
   522→   522→machine-assisted final production - sizing by length, re-washing, colour treatment, assembly, and packing -
   523→   523→against size-wise pricing that ranges from approximately Tk 500/kg for 5-inch hair to Tk 90,000/kg for 30-inch
   524→   524→hair.
   525→   525→Three structural characteristics define the company's risk profile and automation priorities. First, the same lot
   526→   526→number travels with the goods from raw material receipt to finished-goods storage, making lot traceability the
   527→   527→backbone of both quality control and inventory valuation. Second, worker compensation is a hybrid of
   528→   528→grade-based daily rates (A = Tk 90, B = Tk 75, C = Tk 50), attendance bonuses, and performance bonuses, all
   529→   529→of which depend on daily quality grading by four Quality Controllers. Third, the home-based manufacturing
   530→   530→model creates inherent tracking, theft, and skimming risks that the current manual system cannot fully mitigate -

   651→   651→2.2 Span-of-Control and Scaling Mathematics
   652→   652→The Phase 1 distribution hierarchy follows a deliberate 1:5:10:10:10 multiplier. One Production Manager
   653→   653→oversees five Head Leaders, each Head Leader oversees ten Line Leaders, each Line Leader oversees
   654→   654→approximately ten home-based factories, and each factory hosts approximately ten workers (with documented
   655→   655→flexibility of 9, 10, or 11 workers per factory). This mathematical structure means that a single Production
   656→   656→Manager can theoretically coordinate 5 x 10 x 10 x 10 = 5,000 workers, which closely matches the company's
   657→   657→actual workforce. Adding capacity therefore requires adding Head Leaders (each unlocking approximately
   658→   658→1,000 additional workers) rather than restructuring the hierarchy.
   659→   659→Material allocation scales inversely with hierarchy depth. A 1,200 kg washed batch received by the Production
   660→   660→Manager is divided into four to five Head Leader allocations of 200-300 kg each. Each Head Leader divides the
   661→   661→allocation among ten Line Leaders at approximately 30 kg each. Each Line Leader divides the 30 kg across ten
   662→   662→factories at approximately 3 kg per factory. Each factory supervisor divides the 3 kg among ten workers at
   663→   663→100-300 grams per worker per day. This granular allocation is the basis on which shortage and reject tracking is
   664→   664→performed at every return step, and the ERP system must capture each transfer as a discrete inventory
   665→   665→transaction with lot number, quantity, recipient, and timestamp.
   666→   666→2.3 Governance and Approval Authority
   667→   667→Approval authority is concentrated at three levels. The Production Manager approves all material distributions
   668→   668→from the washing plant to Head Leaders and from half-finish returns into the Dhaka final production queue.
   669→   669→Head Leaders approve daily distribution to Line Leaders and authorise worker grade entries in consultation with

   712→   712→3.2 Two-Phase Production Model
   713→   713→The defining architectural feature of the business is the separation of production into two distinct phases. Phase
   714→   714→1 encompasses all operations performed on raw or washed material through the home-based workforce -
   715→   715→hand-tool sorting, opening, classification, and initial bundling. Phase 2 encompasses all machine-assisted and
   716→   716→chemically intensive operations performed at the Dhaka factory - length sizing, re-washing, colour treatment,
   717→   717→assembly, and final packing. This separation exists because the home-based environment cannot accommodate
   718→   718→machines, chemicals, or the specialised training required for final production, while the factory environment
   719→   719→cannot cost-effectively absorb the labour-intensive hand-sorting volume that the home-based model handles at
   720→   720→scale.

   731→   731→4.1 Sourcing Strategy and Supplier Network
   732→   732→Barendra International sources raw human hair from a diversified multi-country supplier network designed to
   733→   733→reduce dependence on any single geography and to access different hair characteristics (colour, texture, length)
   734→   734→native to each region. International sources include India (three suppliers covering the entire country), Pakistan
   735→   735→(one supplier), Uzbekistan, Tajikistan, and Myanmar. The company also procures locally within Bangladesh
   736→   736→through district-level vendors - deliberately not engaging at the root collection level, which would require
   737→   737→building an extensive grassroots collection network. A representative office in India supports direct supplier
   738→   738→relationship management and quality inspection before shipment.
   739→   739→Raw-material pricing varies dramatically by quality tier. Standard import-grade hair is purchased at 1,000-2,000
   740→   740→taka per kilogram, while premium grades reach 12,000-14,000 taka per kilogram. Local hair purchased from
   741→   741→Narayanganj may be approximately 2,000 taka per kilogram cheaper than equivalent Dhaka city material. The
   742→   742→final sized-and-processed product commands radically different prices by length: 8-inch hair sells at
   743→   743→approximately 5,000 taka per kilogram, while 30-inch hair sells at approximately 90,000 taka per kilogram - an
   744→   744→18-times differential that makes length retention during processing the single most important value driver in the
   745→   745→entire pipeline.
   746→   746→4.2 Import Process and Documentation
   747→   747→International procurement is executed through Letters of Credit (LC) issued via the company's bank. The
   748→   748→documentation workflow involves several bank-managed artefacts: SIT numbers (used on the export side and
   749→   749→for dollar tracking), BTC numbers (bank tracking numbers), and LC documents prepared by the bank manager
   750→   750→for the owner's signature. Multiple SIT numbers may be in flight simultaneously, each tracking pending dollar
   751→   751→commitments, encashment status, and applicable export incentives. The bank can prepare invoices covering two
   752→   752→to three parties at a time under a single SIT number, creating a many-to-many relationship between SIT
   753→   753→numbers, suppliers, and LC instruments that the ERP system must model explicitly.
   754→   754→Foreign-currency gain and loss must be recognised on every transaction because purchases are denominated in

   758→   758→Excel-based system handles this manually, which is time-consuming and error-prone; the ERP accounting

   771→   771→5.1 Colour Separation
   772→   772→Upon receipt and initial storage at the Dhaka warehouse, raw material enters the colour separation process. This
   773→   773→is a manual, visually judgement-based operation in which workers physically separate hair by natural colour
   774→   774→into distinct groupings. The industry uses a universal 1-to-10 level system for hair colour (where 1 is black and
   775→   775→10 is lightest blonde), but Barendra International's internal colour codes include specific identifiers such as 600
   776→   776→and 627, which may correspond to either colour codes or supplier lot references. Colour separation is the first
   777→   777→quality-determining step because subsequent processing (washing, chemical treatment) is calibrated to colour
   778→   778→characteristics, and mixing colours at this stage creates defects that cannot be economically recovered later.
   779→   779→5.2 Lot and Batch Number Assignment
   780→   780→After colour separation, each colour-separated batch is assigned a unique lot (or batch) number. Critically, lots
   781→   781→are created based on the goods themselves - the lot is defined by the material it contains, not by an arbitrary
   782→   782→numbering scheme. A lot and the goods it contains can be the same thing. This goods-based lot creation
   783→   783→principle is fundamental to the entire traceability framework because it ensures that every subsequent
   784→   784→transaction, transformation, and movement can be traced back to a specific physical batch of raw material with
   785→   785→known colour, supplier origin, and cost characteristics.
   786→   786→The lot numbering system supports sub-lots: a single main lot (for example, a 120-kilogram batch) can contain
   787→   787→multiple colour-wise sub-lots (for example, colour 600 plus colour 627) under the same main lot number. Lots
   788→   788→can also be merged - two existing lots can be combined into a new lot - or material can be transferred from one
   789→   789→lot to another when physical consolidation makes operational sense. These lot operations must be explicitly
   790→   790→modelled in the ERP system as discrete transactions with audit trails, because the same physical hair may
   791→   791→change lot identity during its lifecycle while remaining fully traceable to its original raw-material receipt.

   825→   825→weight-capture step at the end of washing, with the difference between pre-wash and post-wash quantities

   888→   888→7.4 Shortage, Reject, and Wastage Accounting
   889→   889→At every return step, issued quantity is reconciled against returned quantity, and any variance is classified as
   890→   890→either reject (quality failure - hair that cannot be used) or shortage (unaccounted loss). Industry-standard
   891→   891→wastage is acknowledged as unavoidable - the owner notes that '100 percent of anything cannot be achieved' -
   892→   892→but major losses are flagged for investigation. Typical Dinajpur second-step wastage runs 100-150 grams per
   893→   893→kilogram, and final Dhaka production wastage runs 50-100 grams per kilogram. Reject material must still be
   894→   894→returned with weight recorded, and a recovery rate (for example, 900 grams 'tikal' or recovered per 1,000 grams
   895→   895→input) is calculated for each lot and each factory.
   896→   896→8. Process 5 - Quality Control and Worker Grading
   897→   897→Quality control at Barendra International operates as a continuous, daily activity rather than a discrete
   898→   898→end-of-line inspection. Four Quality Controllers based in Dhaka are responsible for grading the output of all
   899→   899→approximately 5,000 workers every working day, producing the A/B/C grades that directly determine each
   900→   900→worker's daily wage. This daily grading cadence is essential because worker compensation is grade-driven - an
   901→   901→A-grade worker earns 90 taka per day while a C-grade worker earns only 50 taka - and because the home-based
   902→   902→manufacturing model means quality cannot be assured through process controls alone. The QC function
   903→   903→therefore serves as both a quality gate and a payroll input.
   904→   904→8.1 Quality Parameters and Grading Criteria
   905→   905→Worker output is evaluated against four primary quality parameters. Length retention is the most important:
   906→   906→after a standardised pulling test, hair that retains greater length (indicating less breakage) receives a higher
   907→   907→grade. Breakage is directly penalised - workers whose output shows more broken hair receive lower grades
   908→   908→because broken hair has lower commercial value. Colour correctness is visually judged against the lot's colour
   909→   909→specification. Texture and feel are judged by hand, an experiential assessment that the QC cannot reduce to a
   910→   910→fully objective standard. The owner acknowledges that QC judgement is approximately - but not exactly - 70
   911→   911→percent accurate, which is an inherent limitation of the manual grading model.
   912→   912→A critical structural feature of the grading system is lot-quality inheritance: if the raw-material lot itself is of
   913→   913→poor quality, all workers processing that lot may receive C grades regardless of their individual effort or skill.
   914→   914→This creates a fairness tension - workers may be penalised for lot-quality issues outside their control - and the
   915→   915→ERP system should flag lots where the grade distribution is unusually skewed toward C so that management can
   916→   916→distinguish lot-quality effects from worker-performance effects. Without this analytical capability, worker
   917→   917→dissatisfaction and turnover risk increase.

   942→   942→Reject items (hair that fails quality standards) are weighed separately from good output and from shortage
   943→   943→(unaccounted loss). The combined reject-plus-shortage percentage is calculated for each worker, each factory,
   944→   944→and each lot. For example, if 5 kilograms are issued to a factory and 4.9 kilograms are returned, the 100-gram
   945→   945→variance is decomposed into reject (e.g., 10 grams at 0.2 percent) and shortage (e.g., 20 grams), with the
   946→   946→combined loss percentage calculated for management review. Reject material is still physically returned with
   947→   947→weight recorded - it is not discarded at the factory level - because the company may be able to recover value
   948→   948→from lower-grade applications. The ERP system must track reject and shortage as separate inventory transaction
   949→   949→types with distinct cost-account postings.

   981→   981→9.2 Quality Over Quantity Principle
   982→   982→A defining design principle of the compensation system is that quality is rewarded over quantity. The owner
   983→   983→explicitly states that producing 5 kilograms of mediocre output is less valuable than producing 100 grams of
   984→   984→best-quality output, because the profit margin on high-quality hair is dramatically higher. This is why the
   985→   985→monthly bonus is termed a 'performance bonus' rather than a 'production bonus' - the trigger is quality-grade
   986→   986→achievement (A+B percentage exceeding 90 percent) rather than volume produced. The minimum daily output
   987→   987→target of 50 grams exists primarily to identify and weed out workers who are not meeting basic productivity,
   988→   988→with sub-50-gram output triggering an automatic C grade regardless of quality.
   989→   989→9.3 Salary Sheet Approval Workflow
   990→   990→The salary preparation workflow begins with daily summary sheets compiled by the data-entry operators from
   991→   991→QC grading inputs. These daily sheets are aggregated into a monthly salary sheet for each Line Leader's
   992→   992→territory. At month-end, the Head Leader (referred to as the 'bidder' in this context) sits with management to
   993→   993→review the sheets before billing. The review is a quality gate - sheets can be rejected and returned for correction
   994→   994→if errors are found, triggering a rework cycle. Only after the bidder and management concur does the sheet
   995→   995→proceed to the owner for final approval, after which the bKash disbursement is initiated.
   996→   996→9.4 bKash Disbursement and Skimming Risk
   997→   997→Salary disbursement currently flows through bKash mobile payment, but the disbursement model introduces a
   998→   998→structural skimming risk. The approved monthly payout for a single Line Leader's territory (approximately 100
   999→   999→workers) can total approximately 1.5 lakh taka (150,000 taka). This bulk amount is transferred to the Line
  1000→  1000→Leader's bKash account, who is then responsible for distributing the correct individual amounts to each worker.
