   503‚Üí   503‚ÜíExecutive Summary
   504‚Üí   504‚ÜíBarendra International is a Dhaka-headquartered human hair processing and wig manufacturing enterprise that
   505‚Üí   505‚Üíhas emerged as one of Bangladesh's first formal exporters of finished human-hair products. Operating from a
   506‚Üí   506‚Üíhead office in Uttara, Sector 6, Dhaka, and a primary processing factory in Dinajpur (North Bengal), the
   507‚Üí   507‚Üícompany orchestrates a multi-stage production model that combines centralised industrial processing with a
   508‚Üí   508‚Üívast network of approximately 4,000-5,000 home-based female workers. The business imports raw human hair
   509‚Üí   509‚Üífrom India, Pakistan, Uzbekistan, Tajikistan, and Myanmar, supplements supply through local district-level
   510‚Üí   510‚Üívendors, and exports finished wigs and hair products to buyers in the United States, Europe, China, and Africa.
   511‚Üí   511‚ÜíThe company's operations currently run on a combination of Microsoft Excel workbooks, hard-copy vouchers,
   512‚Üí   512‚Üíand a small HR-tracking module, with files centralised on Google Drive. As the workforce has scaled beyond
   513‚Üí   513‚Üí5,000 people and lot-level traceability has become essential for buyer compliance and inventory valuation, the
   514‚Üí   514‚Üíowner has initiated a programme to implement a comprehensive Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) system.
   515‚Üí   515‚ÜíThis document is the foundational business process reference that will guide that ERP implementation across
   516‚Üí   516‚Üífive sequenced phases - beginning with accounting and stock management and culminating in export-import
   517‚Üí   517‚Üíand letter-of-credit (LC) automation.
   518‚Üí   518‚ÜíThe business follows a distinctive two-phase production model. Phase 1 distributes washed, lot-coded raw
   519‚Üí   519‚Üímaterial through a five-tier hierarchy (Production Manager -> Head Leader -> Line Leader -> Home-based
   520‚Üí   520‚ÜíSupervisor -> Worker) where female workers perform hand-tool sorting and classification in their homes. Phase
   521‚Üí   521‚Üí2 receives the half-processed material back at the Dhaka factory, where 100 in-house workers perform
   522‚Üí   522‚Üímachine-assisted final production - sizing by length, re-washing, colour treatment, assembly, and packing -
   523‚Üí   523‚Üíagainst size-wise pricing that ranges from approximately Tk 500/kg for 5-inch hair to Tk 90,000/kg for 30-inch
   524‚Üí   524‚Üíhair.
   525‚Üí   525‚ÜíThree structural characteristics define the company's risk profile and automation priorities. First, the same lot
   526‚Üí   526‚Üínumber travels with the goods from raw material receipt to finished-goods storage, making lot traceability the
   527‚Üí   527‚Üíbackbone of both quality control and inventory valuation. Second, worker compensation is a hybrid of
   528‚Üí   528‚Üígrade-based daily rates (A = Tk 90, B = Tk 75, C = Tk 50), attendance bonuses, and performance bonuses, all
   529‚Üí   529‚Üíof which depend on daily quality grading by four Quality Controllers. Third, the home-based manufacturing
   530‚Üí   530‚Üímodel creates inherent tracking, theft, and skimming risks that the current manual system cannot fully mitigate -
   531‚Üí   531‚Üía single Line Controller may handle bulk payouts of approximately Tk 1.5 lakh for 100 workers, creating
   532‚Üí   532‚Üískimming exposure.

   545‚Üí   545‚Üírecruitment base for the predominantly female home-based workforce.
   546‚Üí   546‚ÜíThe company has been in operation for approximately five to six years and positions itself as Bangladesh's first

   551‚Üí   551‚Üíworkers at the Dhaka final-production floor, and approximately 50-70 supervisory, administrative, and support
   552‚Üí   552‚Üístaff.
   553‚Üí   553‚Üí1.2 Product and Material Characteristics
   554‚Üí   554‚ÜíThe company's sole raw material is real human hair - explicitly not synthetic fibre - which is processed into
   555‚Üí   555‚Üíwigs, hair pieces, extensions, and gentlemen's caps for men. The distinction between real human hair and
   556‚Üí   556‚Üísynthetic substitutes is commercially critical: real hair commands premium pricing (an identical product may
   557‚Üí   557‚Üísell to an African buyer for USD 5-50 but to an American buyer for USD 105, driven by quality perception and
   558‚Üí   558‚Üíend-use), while synthetic substitutes can cause allergic skin reactions and are excluded from the company's
   559‚Üí   559‚Üíproduct range. The company also imports specialised adhesive glue from the United States at USD 4,000-5,000
   560‚Üí   560‚Üíper 100 grams, a material that cannot be substituted with counterfeits without triggering customer allergies.
   561‚Üí   561‚ÜíTwo industry-specific Bengali terms describe the material states that flow through the production pipeline. Buti
   562‚Üí   562‚Üírefers to a tightly bound, fully processed bundle that is ready for final assembly at Dhaka. Kanchi refers to a
   563‚Üí   563‚Üílooser, longer, half-processed bundle that requires further reworking (called remik, or remake) at the Dinajpur
   564‚Üí   564‚Üífacility before it can be promoted to the Buti state. The final wig-assembly stage is termed blended (Bengali:
   565‚Üí   565‚Üíblended). These states - along with raw, washed, distributed, in-factory, half-finish, and finished - constitute the
   566‚Üí   566‚Üíeight inventory buckets that

   570‚Üí   570‚ÜíPlexconcil (Ministry of Commerce) explicitly acknowledges that Myanmar and Bangladesh benefit from lower
   571‚Üí   571‚Üílabour costs and government support, allowing them to process Indian raw hair and sell finished products
   572‚Üí   572‚Üícompetitively. Bangladesh's wig and human-hair exports reached USD 19.16 million in a single fiscal quarter
   573‚Üí   573‚Üíaccording to Export Promotion Bureau data - approximately double the prior-year figure - and the Dinajpur
   574‚Üí   574‚Üídistrict (particularly Chirirbandar upazila) has emerged as a recognised processing hub with 25 or more

   577‚Üí   577‚ÜíBarendra International's competitive position rests on three foundations: access to multi-country raw-material
   578‚Üí   578‚Üísourcing (reducing dependence on any single supplier), Bangladesh's low labour cost advantage (the national
   579‚Üí   579‚Üígarment-sector minimum wage of BDT 12,500 per month, or approximately USD 113, is among the lowest in
   580‚Üí   580‚ÜíAsia), and the scalability of the home-based manufacturing model. However, the company faces growing buyer

   584‚Üí   584‚Üí1.4 Current Systems and Automation Gap
   585‚Üí   585‚ÜíBarendra International currently operates on Microsoft Excel workbooks and hard-copy vouchers, with files
   586‚Üí   586‚Üícentralised on Google Drive and accessed from five to six entry-level laptops at the factory level. Two full-time
   587‚Üí   587‚Üídata-entry operators are dedicated to transcribing the daily output of approximately 5,000 home-based workers.
   588‚Üí   588‚ÜíA small HR tracking module is used in limited fashion to monitor field supervisors' movements, but no
   589‚Üí   589‚Üíintegrated accounting, inventory, production, or payroll system exists. Internet connectivity is available at the
   590‚Üí   590‚ÜíDhaka head office and the Dinajpur factory, with mobile hotspots used in remote field locations.
   591‚Üí   591‚ÜíThe manual system creates four categories of operational pain. First, real-time stock valuation is impossible -
   592‚Üí   592‚Üíthe owner cannot instantaneously answer basic questions such as 'how much stock do I have right now, and
   593‚Üí   593‚Üíwhere is it?' Second, a single transaction currently requires multiple manual entries across cash, party, and bank
   594‚Üí   594‚Üíledgers, creating data-reconciliation overhead and error exposure. Third, the Asset and Liability report - a basic
   595‚Üí   595‚Üímanagement instrument - takes significant time to compile manually. Fourth, lot-level traceability across the
   596‚Üí   596‚Üí40-plus-day production pipeline is extremely difficult to maintain in Excel, undermining both quality
   597‚Üí   597‚Üíinvestigations and buyer compliance. These gaps collectively motivate the phased ERP implementation

   651‚Üí   651‚Üí2.2 Span-of-Control and Scaling Mathematics
   652‚Üí   652‚ÜíThe Phase 1 distribution hierarchy follows a deliberate 1:5:10:10:10 multiplier. One Production Manager
   653‚Üí   653‚Üíoversees five Head Leaders, each Head Leader oversees ten Line Leaders, each Line Leader oversees
   654‚Üí   654‚Üíapproximately ten home-based factories, and each factory hosts approximately ten workers (with documented
   655‚Üí   655‚Üíflexibility of 9, 10, or 11 workers per factory). This mathematical structure means that a single Production
   656‚Üí   656‚ÜíManager can theoretically coordinate 5 x 10 x 10 x 10 = 5,000 workers, which closely matches the company's
   657‚Üí   657‚Üíactual workforce. Adding capacity therefore requires adding Head Leaders (each unlocking approximately
   658‚Üí   658‚Üí1,000 additional workers) rather than restructuring the hierarchy.
   659‚Üí   659‚ÜíMaterial allocation scales inversely with hierarchy depth. A 1,200 kg washed batch received by the Production
   660‚Üí   660‚ÜíManager is divided into four to five Head Leader allocations of 200-300 kg each. Each Head Leader divides the
   661‚Üí   661‚Üíallocation among ten Line Leaders at approximately 30 kg each. Each Line Leader divides the 30 kg across ten
   662‚Üí   662‚Üífactories at approximately 3 kg per factory. Each factory supervisor divides the 3 kg among ten workers at
   663‚Üí   663‚Üí100-300 grams per worker per day. This granular allocation is the basis on which shortage and reject tracking is
   664‚Üí   664‚Üíperformed at every return step, and the ERP system must capture each transfer as a discrete inventory
   665‚Üí   665‚Üítransaction with lot number, quantity, recipient, and timestamp.
   666‚Üí   666‚Üí2.3 Governance and Approval Authority
   667‚Üí   667‚ÜíApproval authority is concentrated at three levels. The Production Manager approves all material distributions
   668‚Üí   668‚Üífrom the washing plant to Head Leaders and from half-finish returns into the Dhaka final production queue.
   669‚Üí   669‚ÜíHead Leaders approve daily distribution to Line Leaders and authorise worker grade entries in consultation with
   670‚Üí   670‚Üíthe Quality Controller. The Owner / Managing Director retains personal approval authority over certain stages -
   671‚Üí   671‚Üíincluding supplier onboarding, large purchase orders, salary sheet final sign-off, and any out-of-tolerance
   672‚Üí   672‚Üíshortage or reject events. This concentration of authority at the owner level is a recognised operational risk (the
   673‚Üí   673‚Üí'owner bottleneck') that the ERP dashboard is intended to mitigate by enabling delegated decision-making with

   712‚Üí   712‚Üí3.2 Two-Phase Production Model
   713‚Üí   713‚ÜíThe defining architectural feature of the business is the separation of production into two distinct phases. Phase
   714‚Üí   714‚Üí1 encompasses all operations performed on raw or washed material through the home-based workforce -
   715‚Üí   715‚Üíhand-tool sorting, opening, classification, and initial bundling. Phase 2 encompasses all machine-assisted and
   716‚Üí   716‚Üíchemically intensive operations performed at the Dhaka factory - length sizing, re-washing, colour treatment,
   717‚Üí   717‚Üíassembly, and final packing. This separation exists because the home-based environment cannot accommodate
   718‚Üí   718‚Üímachines, chemicals, or the specialised training required for final production, while the factory environment
   719‚Üí   719‚Üícannot cost-effectively absorb the labour-intensive hand-sorting volume that the home-based model handles at
   720‚Üí   720‚Üíscale.
   721‚Üí   721‚ÜíThe two-phase model creates a critical intermediate inventory state - half-finished goods - that must be tracked
   722‚Üí   722‚Üíwith the same lot identity as the original raw material. Material can spend one to two weeks in Phase 1
   723‚Üí   723‚Üí(distributed across dozens of home-based factories) before being returned to Dhaka for Phase 2. During this
   724‚Üí   724‚Üíperiod, the ERP system must maintain visibility of every kilogram by lot, by Line Leader, by factory, and by
   725‚Üí   725‚Üíindividual worker, and must reconcile issued quantities against returned quantities with shortage and reject
   726‚Üí   726‚Üíaccounting at each level of the hierarchy.

   730‚Üí   730‚Üí4. Process 1 - Raw Material Procurement
   731‚Üí   731‚Üí4.1 Sourcing Strategy and Supplier Network
   732‚Üí   732‚ÜíBarendra International sources raw human hair from a diversified multi-country supplier network designed to
   733‚Üí   733‚Üíreduce dependence on any single geography and to access different hair characteristics (colour, texture, length)
   734‚Üí   734‚Üínative to each region. International sources include India (three suppliers covering the entire country), Pakistan
   735‚Üí   735‚Üí(one supplier), Uzbekistan, Tajikistan, and Myanmar. The company also procures locally within Bangladesh
   736‚Üí   736‚Üíthrough district-level vendors - deliberately not engaging at the root collection level, which would require
   737‚Üí   737‚Üíbuilding an extensive grassroots collection network. A representative office in India supports direct supplier
   738‚Üí   738‚Üírelationship management and quality inspection before shipment.
   739‚Üí   739‚ÜíRaw-material pricing varies dramatically by quality tier. Standard import-grade hair is purchased at 1,000-2,000
   740‚Üí   740‚Üítaka per kilogram, while premium grades reach 12,000-14,000 taka per kilogram. Local hair purchased from
   741‚Üí   741‚ÜíNarayanganj may be approximately 2,000 taka per kilogram cheaper than equivalent Dhaka city material. The
   742‚Üí   742‚Üífinal sized-and-processed product commands radically different prices by length: 8-inch hair sells at
   743‚Üí   743‚Üíapproximately 5,000 taka per kilogram, while 30-inch hair sells at approximately 90,000 taka per kilogram - an
   744‚Üí   744‚Üí18-times differential that makes length retention during processing the single most important value driver in the
   745‚Üí   745‚Üíentire pipeline.
   746‚Üí   746‚Üí4.2 Import Process and Documentation
   747‚Üí   747‚ÜíInternational procurement is executed through Letters of Credit (LC) issued via the company's bank. The
   748‚Üí   748‚Üídocumentation workflow involves several bank-managed artefacts: SIT numbers (used on the export side and
   749‚Üí   749‚Üífor dollar tracking), BTC numbers (bank tracking numbers), and LC documents prepared by the bank manager
   750‚Üí   750‚Üífor the owner's signature. Multiple SIT numbers may be in flight simultaneously, each tracking pending dollar
   751‚Üí   751‚Üícommitments, encashment status, and applicable export incentives. The bank can prepare invoices covering two
   752‚Üí   752‚Üíto three parties at a time under a single SIT number, creating a many-to-many relationship between SIT
   753‚Üí   753‚Üínumbers, suppliers, and LC instruments that the ERP system must model explicitly.
   754‚Üí   754‚ÜíForeign-currency gain and loss must be recognised on every transaction because purchases are denominated in
   755‚Üí   755‚Üíforeign currency (primarily US dollars) while functional currency is Bangladeshi taka. The applicable bank rate
   756‚Üí   756‚Üíon the transaction date determines the initial recorded cost, and any subsequent exchange-rate movement
   757‚Üí   757‚Üíbetween commitment and encashment creates a gain or loss that must be captured automatically. The current
   758‚Üí   758‚ÜíExcel-based system handles this manually, which is time-consuming and error-prone; the ERP accounting
   759‚Üí   759‚Üímodule (Phase 1) is specifically designed to automate this recognition.

   771‚Üí   771‚Üí5.1 Colour Separation
   772‚Üí   772‚ÜíUpon receipt and initial storage at the Dhaka warehouse, raw material enters the colour separation process. This
   773‚Üí   773‚Üíis a manual, visually judgement-based operation in which workers physically separate hair by natural colour
   774‚Üí   774‚Üíinto distinct groupings. The industry uses a universal 1-to-10 level system for hair colour (where 1 is black and
   775‚Üí   775‚Üí10 is lightest blonde), but Barendra International's internal colour codes include specific identifiers such as 600
   776‚Üí   776‚Üíand 627, which may correspond to either colour codes or supplier lot references. Colour separation is the first
   777‚Üí   777‚Üíquality-determining step because subsequent processing (washing, chemical treatment) is calibrated to colour
   778‚Üí   778‚Üícharacteristics, and mixing colours at this stage creates defects that cannot be economically recovered later.
   779‚Üí   779‚Üí5.2 Lot and Batch Number Assignment
   780‚Üí   780‚ÜíAfter colour separation, each colour-separated batch is assigned a unique lot (or batch) number. Critically, lots
   781‚Üí   781‚Üíare created based on the goods themselves - the lot is defined by the material it contains, not by an arbitrary
   782‚Üí   782‚Üínumbering scheme. A lot and the goods it contains can be the same thing. This goods-based lot creation
   783‚Üí   783‚Üíprinciple is fundamental to the entire traceability framework because it ensures that every subsequent
   784‚Üí   784‚Üítransaction, transformation, and movement can be traced back to a specific physical batch of raw material with
   785‚Üí   785‚Üíknown colour, supplier origin, and cost characteristics.
   786‚Üí   786‚ÜíThe lot numbering system supports sub-lots: a single main lot (for example, a 120-kilogram batch) can contain
   787‚Üí   787‚Üímultiple colour-wise sub-lots (for example, colour 600 plus colour 627) under the same main lot number. Lots
   788‚Üí   788‚Üícan also be merged - two existing lots can be combined into a new lot - or material can be transferred from one
   789‚Üí   789‚Üílot to another when physical consolidation makes operational sense. These lot operations must be explicitly
   790‚Üí   790‚Üímodelled in the ERP system as discrete transactions with audit trails, because the same physical hair may
   791‚Üí   791‚Üíchange lot identity during its lifecycle while remaining fully traceable to its original raw-material receipt.

   806‚Üí   806‚Üí6. Process 3 - Washing and Preparation
   807‚Üí   807‚ÜíAfter lot creation in Dhaka, material is transported to the Dinajpur factory - the company's primary industrial
   808‚Üí   808‚Üíprocessing facility - for washing and preparation. The Dinajpur site houses the washing plant (staffed by 5-7
   809‚Üí   809‚Üípeople including a floor manager and washing-plant lead), accounts staff (5 people), and 200 second-step
   810‚Üí   810‚Üíproduction workers. The washing process is the first industrial transformation applied to the raw material and
   811‚Üí   811‚Üíserves multiple purposes: it removes impurities accumulated during collection and transit, prepares the hair for
   812‚Üí   812‚Üícolour separation consistency, and establishes the baseline weight against which all subsequent shortage and
   813‚Üí   813‚Üíreject calculations are measured.
   814‚Üí   814‚Üí6.1 Wash, Dry, and Restock Cycle
   815‚Üí   815‚ÜíThe washing cycle comprises four sequential steps. First, the hair is washed using shampoo and chemical
   816‚Üí   816‚Üítreatments calibrated to the colour and quality characteristics recorded at lot creation. Second, the washed hair
   817‚Üí   817‚Üíis dried using controlled methods to prevent damage and to standardise moisture content. Third, the dried hair is
   818‚Üí   818‚Üíreweighed and restocked with a new recorded quantity. Fourth, the restocked material is forwarded to the
   819‚Üí   819‚ÜíProduction Manager for distribution into Phase 1 field manufacturing.
   820‚Üí   820‚ÜíWeight loss during washing is a normal and expected outcome - typically 2-3 kilograms per batch - driven by
   821‚Üí   821‚Üíthe removal of impurities, moisture loss, and minor processing waste. This weight loss must be recorded as a
   822‚Üí   822‚Üíformal inventory adjustment against the lot, with the new restocked quantity becoming the baseline for all
   823‚Üí   823‚Üísubsequent distribution and reconciliation. Failure to capture this adjustment accurately creates phantom
   824‚Üí   824‚Üíshortages downstream that cannot be explained or resolved. The ERP system must therefore enforce a
   825‚Üí   825‚Üíweight-capture step at the end of washing, with the difference between pre-wash and post-wash quantities
   826‚Üí   826‚Üíautomatically calculated and posted to a washing-loss account.
